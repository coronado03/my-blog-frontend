# When the Craft Starts Talking Back

Lately, I've been going through some things.

I've been spiraling not in a dramatic Matthew McConaughey type of way. The kind where you keep thinking about the same thing from slightly different angles, the kind that makes you binge eat and fall into bad habits.

And for me, that thing has been... TECH. The state of it. The direction it’s heading. And where WE fit into all of it.

## The Identity Problem I Didn’t Expect

I’ve spent a lot of time learning how to code.

Not casually. Not just enough to get by. I mean really trying to sharpen the skill. From building cool projects to solving leetcode and reading tech literature.

And then AI showed up in a way that felt different. At the beginning I didn't think it could compare to me but in 2026 I felt a shift.

It was no longer as a tool I could ignore. Not as something experimental or niche. But as something that could do *a lot* of what I do but quickly, confidently, and without getting tired.

That’s where my discomfort started.

Because it forces a weird question:

> If a machine can do what I’ve spent years learning… then what exactly is *my* value?

It’s not a fun question. And it doesn’t have a clean answer.

## Work Is Work

After going in circles for a while, I landed somewhere practical.

Work is work. My worth/identity is not linked to my work.

At the end of the day, if AI helps me deliver better results, faster results, or just makes me more effective then it makes no sense to fight it. This is the direction things are going. Ignoring it doesn’t preserve anything.

So I’ve decided to lean into it *professionally*.

Use the tools. Understand them. Integrate them. Let them handle the parts they’re good at so I can focus on higher-level thinking, decisions, and outcomes.

Not because I love it, but beacause in reality it works.

## But Coding Was Never Just Work

Here’s the part that took me longer to admit:

Coding was never just about output for me.

It wasn’t just about shipping features or getting a quick buck from a quick Saas.

I like the process. When I realized I could code and get paid a really good salary, I was dumbfounded. Honestly, I’d do it for free.

I like writing something messy and refining it until it feels right. I like understanding *why* something works, not just that it does.

It’s a craft. No different from playing an instrument

And crafts don’t lose their meaning just because they can be automated. I bet I could make an AI song but it won't fulfill my soul the same way hours of guitar practice would.

## Drawing a Line (For Myself)

So I made a decision that feels simple, but also kind of important.

In my work life:  
I’ll use AI freely. I’ll treat it as leverage. I’ll optimize for results.

In my personal time:  I won’t.

Not as some "ANTI-AI" stance but because I don't code for the result but for the process.

I want the experience of figuring it out.

The friction is part of it. The confusion is part of it. Even the frustration is part of it.

## Maybe That’s Enough

I don’t think this resolves the bigger questions.

AI isn’t going anywhere. As much as I'd like to cope by saying this is a bubble that will burst and everything will go back to normal, that's delusional.

But for now, this split feels right. Let AI handle the work, I'll sharpen my craft.

And maybe that’s enough to keep moving forward without feeling like I’m losing something important along the way.
